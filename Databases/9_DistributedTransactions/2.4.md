# 🌐 2.4 DISTRIBUTED TRANSACTIONS (2PC, SAGAS)

---

## 📘 Definition (textbook-style)

A **distributed transaction** is a transaction that spans multiple independent systems or database nodes and ensures all of them either commit or rollback together.

---

## 💡 Intuition (Why it exists)

Single DB transaction → easy

But in real systems:

👉 Example:

* Payment service (DB1)
* Order service (DB2)

Flow:

1. Deduct money
2. Create order

👉 What if:

* Payment succeeds
* Order fails

❌ System becomes inconsistent

> Need: **atomicity across multiple systems**

---

## 🧩 Core Problem

> “How do we ensure all systems agree on commit or rollback?”

---

# 🔁 1. TWO-PHASE COMMIT (2PC)

---

## 📘 Definition

A protocol that ensures all participating nodes either commit or abort a transaction in a coordinated manner.

---

## 🧩 Components

* **Coordinator** (orchestrates)
* **Participants** (databases/services)

---

## 🔄 Flow (VERY IMPORTANT)

---

### Phase 1: Prepare Phase

1. Coordinator asks all participants:

   > “Can you commit?”

2. Each participant:

   * Writes to WAL
   * Locks resources
   * Responds:

     * YES (ready)
     * NO (fail)

---

### Phase 2: Commit Phase

* If ALL say YES → commit
* If ANY say NO → rollback

---

## 🧠 Key Insight

> 2PC ensures atomicity across systems

---

## ⚖️ Trade-offs

---

### ✅ Pros

* Strong consistency
* Atomic across systems

---

### ❌ Cons (CRITICAL)

---

### 1. Blocking

* If coordinator crashes → system stuck

---

### 2. High Latency

* Multiple round trips

---

### 3. Not scalable

* Locks resources across systems

---

## 🌍 Real-world Mapping

* Traditional distributed DBs
* Enterprise systems

👉 Rare in modern large-scale systems

---

# 🔄 2. SAGAS (MODERN APPROACH)

---

## 📘 Definition

A **Saga** is a sequence of local transactions where each step has a corresponding **compensating action** to undo it if needed.

---

## 💡 Core Idea

> “Instead of rollback, do compensating actions”

---

## 🔄 Example (Order system)

---

### Step-by-step

1. Payment service → deduct money
2. Order service → create order

---

### If failure happens:

👉 Instead of rollback:

* Execute compensating action
* Refund money

---

## 🧠 Key Insight

> Sagas = eventual consistency + compensation

---

## 🧩 Types of Sagas

---

### 1. Orchestrated Saga

* Central coordinator controls flow

---

### 2. Choreographed Saga

* Services emit events
* No central control

---

## 🌍 Real-world Mapping

* Microservices architectures
* Event-driven systems

---

## ⚖️ 2PC vs Sagas (VERY IMPORTANT)

| Feature          | 2PC      | Sagas                |
| ---------------- | -------- | -------------------- |
| Consistency      | Strong   | Eventual             |
| Latency          | High     | Lower                |
| Scalability      | Poor     | High                 |
| Failure handling | Rollback | Compensating actions |

---

## 🎯 When to use what (Decision Thinking)

---

### Use 2PC when:

* Strong consistency required
* Small system
* Low latency not critical

---

### Use Sagas when:

* Microservices architecture
* High scale
* Eventual consistency acceptable

---

## 🧠 Key Insight (INTERVIEW GOLD)

> “At scale, distributed transactions using 2PC are avoided due to blocking and latency — modern systems use sagas with compensating actions for better scalability.”

---

## 🗣️ Interview One-liner

> “Distributed transactions ensure atomicity across systems, typically using 2PC for strong consistency or sagas for scalable eventual consistency.”

---

## 🚨 Red Flags / Mistakes

* ❌ Using 2PC in high-scale systems
* ❌ Ignoring failure scenarios
* ❌ Not designing compensating actions
* ❌ Assuming rollback always possible
* ❌ Not handling partial failures

---

## 🔥 Advanced Insight (VERY IMPORTANT)

👉 Real systems:

* Avoid distributed transactions
* Design for:

  * Idempotency
  * Retries
  * Eventual consistency

---

## ✍️ Quick Mental Model

> 2PC → lock + commit everywhere
> Saga → do + undo if needed

---

## 🔗 Connection

* CAP → trade-offs
* Replication → consistency
* Transactions → correctness

👉 Distributed transactions combine ALL of these
