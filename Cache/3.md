## **Cache Eviction, Expiry & Invalidation.md**

---

## **3.1 Eviction Policies**

When cache is full → **what to remove?**

### LRU (Least Recently Used)

* Removes least recently accessed item
* Works well with temporal locality

**Pros**

* Simple, effective in most cases

**Cons**

* Fails with cyclic access patterns

---

### LFU (Least Frequently Used)

* Removes least frequently accessed items

**Pros**

* Keeps consistently popular items

**Cons**

* Slow to adapt to changing trends

---

### FIFO (First In First Out)

* Oldest entry removed

**Pros**

* Very simple

**Cons**

* Ignores access patterns → poor performance

---

### Random

* Random eviction

**Pros**

* Low overhead

**Cons**

* Unpredictable

---

### ARC (Adaptive Replacement Cache)

* Combines LRU + LFU

**Pros**

* Handles both recency & frequency

**Cons**

* Complex, rarely used in interviews

---

**Interview Line:**

> “LRU is the default choice due to simplicity and good real-world performance.”

---

## **3.2 TTL & Expiration**

### Hard TTL

* Cache entry expires after fixed time

**Example:**

* User profile cached for 5 minutes

---

### Soft TTL

* Serve stale data, refresh in background

**Used for:**

* High availability systems

---

### Sliding TTL

* Expiry resets on access

**Good for:**

* Frequently accessed data

---

**Tradeoff:**

* Low TTL → fresh but low hit rate
* High TTL → stale but high hit rate

---

## **3.3 Cache Invalidation Strategies**

> “There are only two hard things: cache invalidation and naming things.”

---

### Write Invalidate (Most Common)

**Flow:**

* Update DB
* Delete cache entry

**Pros**

* Simple
* Avoids stale data buildup

**Cons**

* Next read = cache miss

---

### Write Update

**Flow:**

* Update DB
* Update cache

**Pros**

* Cache always fresh

**Cons**

* More complex
* Risk of inconsistency

---

### Event-Driven Invalidation

**Flow:**

* DB change → event → invalidate cache

**Example tools:**

* Kafka-based invalidation pipelines

**Pros**

* Scales well

**Cons**

* Event delays → temporary inconsistency

---

## **3.4 Cache Consistency Models**

### Strong Consistency

* Cache always reflects latest DB state

**Achieved via:**

* Write-through
* Synchronous updates

---

### Eventual Consistency

* Cache may be stale temporarily

**Most real systems use this**

---

### Read-After-Write Consistency

**Problem:**
User updates profile → immediately reads → sees old data

**Solutions:**

* Bypass cache temporarily
* Update cache on write
* Use versioning

---

## **3.5 Versioning & Cache Keys**

### Cache Key Design (VERY IMPORTANT)

Bad:

```
user_123
```

Better:

```
user:v1:123
```

---

### Namespacing

* Avoid collisions
* Group related data

---

### Versioning

* Increment version → invalidate all old entries

**Example:**

```
feed:v2:user:123
```

---

**Interview Insight:**

> “Versioning is a powerful way to invalidate entire datasets without scanning cache.”

---

## **3.6 Multi-node Invalidation (Distributed Systems)**

### Problem

* Multiple cache nodes → one node updates → others stale

---

### Solutions

#### 1. Broadcast Invalidation

* Send invalidation to all nodes

**Cons:** not scalable

---

#### 2. Pub/Sub (Common)

* Publish invalidation event
* All nodes subscribe

**Example:**

* Redis Pub/Sub

---

#### 3. Centralized Cache

* Single cache layer

**Tradeoff:**

* Simpler consistency vs scalability bottleneck

---

## **Key Tradeoffs (Interview Focus)**

| Aspect      | Option A     | Option B         |
| ----------- | ------------ | ---------------- |
| Freshness   | Write update | Write invalidate |
| Performance | High TTL     | Low TTL          |
| Complexity  | Simple TTL   | Event-driven     |

---

## **Common Failure Scenarios**

### 1. Stale Data

* Missing invalidation
* High TTL

---

### 2. Cache Stampede (Intro)

* Many requests hit DB on expiry

---

### 3. Split-Brain Cache

* Nodes out of sync

---

## **Interview Playbook**

When asked:

> “How do you handle cache invalidation?”

Answer:

1. “On write, I’ll update DB and invalidate cache”
2. “Use TTL as fallback”
3. “For distributed setup, use pub/sub invalidation”
4. “If scale increases, consider versioning”

---

## **Golden Rule**

> “Cache invalidation strategy is more important than choosing the cache itself.”
